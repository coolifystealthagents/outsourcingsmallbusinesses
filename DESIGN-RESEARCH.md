# Design research

## Brief

OutsourcingSmallBusinesses.com needs to help an owner decide what to hand off before the hiring conversation starts. The public offer is limited to Filipino talent recruited and hired in the Philippines. The page cannot lean on rates, savings claims, client logos, testimonials, or invented staffing numbers.

Research was completed from desktop homepage captures on July 21, 2026. The review focused on first-screen hierarchy, visual language, the main conversion path, and how each competitor makes an intangible staffing service feel concrete.

## Five direct competitors

### 1. Outsource Access

The homepage uses a purple hero, yellow calls to action, a CEO video, awards, and long explanatory copy. Its process-documentation pitch is useful because it turns staffing into an operating system rather than a loose introduction. The first screen is crowded, though. The navigation, webinar bar, video, cookie panel, chat panel, and several actions all compete.

Takeaway: show how work gets documented, but do it with one clear artifact and one clear action.

### 2. Remote CoWorker

The strongest first screen in the set uses a warm cream canvas, a direct headline, a visible primary action, and a person surrounded by small work cards. The composition explains the service before the visitor reads every line. Ratings and media marks add density, and the layered cards are close to becoming too busy.

Takeaway: pair a human face with a restrained view of the work. Keep the proof honest and the action consistent.

### 3. Virtual Coworker

A navy and gold hero states the Philippines focus immediately and puts consultation and plan links side by side. The employee cutout adds energy, while badges sit close to the decision point. The header is crowded, and the copy asks one hero to carry too many ideas at once.

Takeaway: state Filipino talent plainly. Use fewer claims and a calmer image treatment.

### 4. Wing Assistant

Wing uses a bright blue-to-purple hero, large geometric type, assistant portraits, and floating quality and speed badges. Its managed-service distinction is sharp: the visitor understands that this is not a freelancer marketplace. The volume of superlatives, numbers, and famous logos makes the first screen feel promotional.

Takeaway: explain who owns quality and follow-up, but avoid unsupported rankings, metrics, and brand associations.

### 5. 20four7VA

The site puts contact details, award marks, savings figures, a long headline, and several conversion routes into the opening screen. Blue and orange give the actions contrast, but the stacked bars, all-caps type, cookie panel, chat prompt, and multiple calls to action make it hard to know where to look.

Takeaway: keep contact easy to find, then remove everything that competes with the main decision.

## Winner: Remote CoWorker

Remote CoWorker has the best underlying direction for this brief. Its warm palette feels approachable to a small-business owner, and its human-plus-workflow composition makes remote support easier to understand. It also keeps the service and action visible without forcing the visitor to decode an abstract brand promise.

The redesign adapts that logic, not its assets or layout. The original direction is an owner's handoff desk: a cream editorial canvas, a local illustrative workspace photo, a compact task board, and a visible line between delegated work and owner-only decisions. Coral, dark ink, and teal replace the competitor palette. The board geometry, copy, logo, role brief, section order, and all page assets are original.

## Chosen homepage system

- Marker: `small-business-handoff-2026`
- H1: `Hand off the busywork. Keep your hands on the business.`
- Dominant artifact: an owner's handoff board with one workspace image, three recurring work lanes, and an owner-approval line
- Primary action: `Build my role plan`
- Location language: Philippines and Filipino talent only
- Typography: Fraunces for owner-led editorial headings, Manrope for practical UI and body copy
- Palette: warm paper, dark ink, coral action, teal status, pale blue-gray structure
- Shape language: clipped paper corners, sturdy cards, thin rule lines, and modest rounding
- Proof policy: operating details only; no public pricing, savings claims, fake reviews, client logos, rankings, or invented numbers

## Image and logo

The homepage uses a local 1600-pixel Pexels workspace image as an illustrative professional scene. The alt text describes what is visible and does not assign a nationality to the pictured person. The surrounding copy, not the person's appearance, establishes the Philippines-only model.

The new SVG logo is an original checklist card with a green check and a small plus marker. It reflects a practical role-planning desk rather than an anonymous staffing marketplace.
